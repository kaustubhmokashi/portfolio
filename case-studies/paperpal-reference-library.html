Case Study

Paperpal Reference Library

A reference management experience for academics that converts uploaded files into structured citations, exports them to formats like BibTeX, and syncs with tools such as Zotero so citations can be reused across Paperpal, ChatPDF, Word, and Docs.

Why

It started with a familiar moment I kept hearing in interviews. A researcher opens a draft the night before a submission, sees a handful of citations marked in yellow, and realizes the references list does not match the latest version of the paper. Nothing is technically broken. The file exists. The PDF is in a folder. The reference manager has a copy. But the paper is still missing a volume number, an author order is reversed, or the citation style does not match the journal template. It is not a big error, but it creates a panic spiral because the last mile matters most.

As we looked closer, the story was consistent across roles. A doctoral student is juggling drafts between Word and Docs, exporting BibTeX, then importing it into a reference manager, then back into the writing tool. A research assistant is helping their lead by cleaning citations, but their updates live in a different tool so nothing stays in sync. A principal investigator has a library of years of citations and is reluctant to move away from a tool that works, even if it is slow. Everyone is moving, but the references are always lagging a step behind.

The friction did not come from a lack of tooling. It came from how the tools were stitched together. When a paper or reference lives in multiple places, the act of correcting it becomes a game of telephone. Each export or sync is a chance to lose context. Most teams were using some combination of Zotero, a cloud drive, and a writing surface, and they had built a personal set of workarounds to keep things correct. Those workarounds were invisible to anyone outside the team, which meant the cost of maintaining references was carried silently by the people doing the work.

The biggest insight for us was that the pain was emotional as much as it was procedural. References are proof. They are the academic equivalent of receipts. When they are out of sync, it shakes confidence in the entire body of work. The researcher does not only worry about formatting. They worry about credibility. They worry about trust. They worry about whether the paper is ready to be read. That stress shows up in the hours before a deadline and in the weeks after a revision, when the library is still not stable.

We also saw how simple actions created unexpected drift. A file upload happens in one place. A citation is pulled in another. A metadata correction is made in a third. Even when tools claimed to sync, the sync was often delayed or partial, so the person doing the work could not rely on it. The more cross tool the workflow became, the more brittle the reference set felt. It was not a single dramatic failure, but a thousand tiny breaks that accumulated into loss of trust.

That is why we framed the problem as a continuity problem, not a storage problem. We were not trying to make a new library for the sake of it. We were trying to make a place where references could be treated as a stable asset. That stability had to travel across tools, across formats, and across collaborators. The goal was a library that reduced the hidden labor of keeping citations correct, so researchers could focus on the work rather than the maintenance.

The product goal became clear: design a reference library that behaves like a reliable backbone. It should accept messy real world inputs, convert them into clean structured references, and then keep those references consistent wherever they are used. That meant the library had to be more than a place to drop files. It had to be a reference system that protects trust through the entire research workflow.

  • Uploaded papers did not reliably auto-generate structured references.
  • Export formats like BibTeX and RIS required manual cleanup and reordering.
  • Reference managers fell out of sync when edits happened in other tools.
  • Citations were hard to reuse across Paperpal, ChatPDF, Word, and Docs.

The target outcome was to make reference creation, export, and reuse feel like a single continuous flow instead of a set of disconnected tasks. That meant designing a library that treated references as a living system: a place where files, metadata, and citations stay tied together and can travel with the researcher wherever the writing happens.

What

To define what we should build, we spent time living inside the existing workflow. That meant watching how people actually move across tools, not how they say they do it. We ran interviews and quick shadowing sessions with researchers, editorial teams, and product specialists who help authors prepare papers for submission. We captured the moments where the workflow broke: uploads that did not parse, metadata that was missing, and formatting steps that felt like a black box. We mapped these steps visually so the team could see how many switches happened before a reference was ready to use.

We also did a wide review of competitor tools, from full scale reference managers to lighter weight citation helpers. The pattern was clear. Tools were good at storage and export, but they were less confident about continuity across surfaces. Some provided a browser extension, others provided a sync, but the user still had to make decisions about where the source of truth lived. The more complex the workflow, the more fragile it became. That fragility showed up in subtle errors and in the time cost of checking everything after the fact.

This research gave us a set of guiding principles. First, references should be created immediately, with clear signals that parsing has succeeded or needs help. Second, references should be editable where they live. Users should not need to jump to another tool just to fix a small field. Third, export and sync should be part of the same moment as creation, not an afterthought. Fourth, the library should behave like a trusted spine that can be used across Paperpal, ChatPDF, Word, and Docs without reformatting the same reference repeatedly.

We then translated those principles into outcomes. The library needed to be an ingestion engine that converts uploads into clean references automatically. It had to support multiple export formats and make those formats feel native, not like add ons. And it needed to stay in sync with external systems such as Zotero without forcing the user to choose a main reference manager. Most importantly, citations pulled from the library had to stay consistent across every surface.

The word "library" can sound passive, so we framed it as a workspace. A library implies storage. A workspace implies action. We wanted researchers to feel that this space was active: it parses, corrects, exports, and syncs. That framing helped us decide how to prioritize functions. The top of the page needed to make creation feel safe and fast. The center needed to make scanning and verification feel easy. The right rail needed to make export and sync feel immediate rather than buried.

We also made some deliberate tradeoffs. We did not try to rebuild the full power of a reference manager in one release. Instead we focused on the most common task: turning files into references and then reusing those references without manual repair. That focus allowed us to ship a strong core without creating feature bloat. The goal was not to replace every tool. The goal was to remove the most painful steps and keep references trustworthy inside the Paperpal ecosystem.

  • Auto-generate structured references from uploaded files.
  • Enable exports in BibTeX, RIS, and other standard citation formats.
  • Sync references and files with Zotero as a first‑class action.
  • Surface library citations across Paperpal, ChatPDF, Word, and Docs.
  • Make corrections and edits propagate back to the reference record.

This phase also shaped the language of the system. We intentionally framed the library as a reference workspace rather than a static file shelf. The vocabulary (sources, citations, export, sync) was chosen to align with how academics already think about their work, which made the feature feel familiar even though the workflows were being rebuilt from scratch.

Competitor Analysis

Competitor review clarified where people were already comfortable and where the pain lived. Most tools handled bulk storage and citation export, but they treated sync and multi‑surface reuse as secondary. We cataloged how each tool dealt with ingestion, metadata correction, and export workflows, then mapped gaps around cross‑tool continuity. The most frequent failure points were inconsistent metadata parsing and the lack of a clear “source of truth” across tools.

How

We structured the solution around three moments: ingestion, reference editing, and reuse. That structure let us build the experience like a story with a beginning, middle, and end, instead of a loose collection of tools. It also gave each part of the interface a clear job to do. Ingestion is about trust, editing is about accuracy, and reuse is about momentum.

Ingestion needed to feel immediate and dependable. We designed the upload flow to show recognition states, file type identification, and parsing feedback so users could see what the system was doing, not just wait for it. If the system was still extracting metadata, we surfaced that state. If it succeeded, we showed the result instantly. If it needed help, we positioned the correction right next to the extracted fields. The goal was to make the system feel confident and transparent, because that is what earns trust in a workflow that depends on accuracy.

Reference editing needed to be a review pass, not a separate mode. People told us they wanted to fix references without feeling like they were leaving the library. So we designed inline edits that sit within the list view. Author order, publication year, title formatting, and source type can be corrected in place. The UI keeps the line item stable while fields update, which means the user can scan, edit, and move on without losing their place. We also used subtle visual cues to show what is verified versus what needs attention, which helps the researcher prioritize without reading every field.

Reuse became the distribution layer. We placed export, sync, and citation access in a dedicated action zone so it is always visible. The right rail acts like a control center: it shows available formats, offers quick export, and highlights the sync status with tools like Zotero. This approach prevents the common trap of burying export inside menus, which creates a disconnect between creation and use. The moment a reference is created, the system makes it clear how it can travel.

The dashboard layout supports that loop. The left navigation organizes sources and filters without hiding the act of creation. The center area is optimized for scanning references at scale, while the right rail becomes the action surface for export, sync, and citation tools. This layout keeps the library visually stable even when different file types are being uploaded, while giving researchers clear signals about what is ready, what needs review, and what is already synced.

We designed the information architecture to minimize mode switching. Search, filters, and collections are visible but not dominant. The goal was to make the list of references the hero, since that is where researchers spend most of their time. We kept the density high but the visual hierarchy clear: primary metadata is strong, secondary metadata is quieter, and actions are aligned to the right for quick access. This makes the list feel like a clean inventory rather than a noisy table.

We also baked in language and microcopy that feels academic without feeling heavy. The action labels use verbs like export, sync, cite, and review. We avoided technical terms that only make sense to reference management experts. The result is a system that feels familiar to long time researchers but approachable to students who are building their first reference set.

The most important part of this implementation was the relationship between data and trust. Every visual element is designed to answer a simple question: can I rely on this reference? Progress states show when parsing is done. Edit states show when a field has been changed. Sync states show when an external tool is up to date. These signals keep the user grounded. They reduce the fear that a reference is out of date or that a citation was copied from a stale source.

Finally, we made sure the library works as a shared foundation across the Paperpal ecosystem. Citations pulled in ChatPDF or writing surfaces are tied back to the same reference record, so the user does not need to rebuild the same citation in multiple places. That creates a sense of continuity that was missing in the earlier workflow. The story ends where it began: researchers trust that their references will hold, which lets them focus on the research itself.

  • Upload → parse → reference creation with progress states.
  • Editable reference cards to fix metadata and author order.
  • Export formats and Zotero sync from a unified action panel.
  • Consistent citation picker used across Paperpal, ChatPDF, and Docs.
  • Library filters for file type, status, and collection.

Outcome & Next Steps

The reference library establishes a consistent foundation for citation work across the Paperpal ecosystem. The next layer is to expand collaboration signals (shared collections, team ownership, and version history) while refining automated extraction accuracy for multi‑author and multi‑volume publications. As the system scales, the goal is to make references feel as reliable as the documents they were derived from.
